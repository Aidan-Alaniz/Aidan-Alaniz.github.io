## Overview

As part of the ASCTE 301/401 BioDesign project, I designed a theoretical organism engineered to break down plastic pollution in the ocean at a cellular level. The project involved researching existing plastic-degrading biology, identifying the limitations of natural processes, and proposing a modified organism to address them.

## Goals

- Research the science behind plastic degradation in natural environments
- Identify which types of plastic are most damaging and most feasible to target
- Design an organism with the biological mechanisms needed to break down plastic efficiently in an ocean environment

## Approach

The design focused on engineering a microorganism capable of producing enzymes that break down common ocean plastics — particularly PET — into harmless byproducts. The proposal addressed the organism's survivability in a saltwater environment, rate of degradation, and ecological safety considerations.

## Tools & Skills Used

- **Biology & Chemistry** — enzyme function, cellular mechanics, plastic polymer structure
- **Research** — scientific literature review and synthesis
- **Engineering Design** — applying constraints and tradeoffs to a biological system

## What I Learned

This project pushed me to think like an engineer in a domain that isn't traditionally engineering. Designing something at the cellular level means every decision has cascading effects — changing one mechanism affects survivability, degradation rate, and environmental impact simultaneously. It gave me a new appreciation for how complex biological systems really are.
